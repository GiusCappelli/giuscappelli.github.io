The Bottleneck of Blind Execution

Acting before thinking subtly slows down work instead of accelearting it. It should be reasoned out.

August 12, 2025

Say you have to write a PhD thesis in very little time. You have agreed on an outline with your supervisor(-s in my case), and now you have to pump out section after section, chapter after chapter. You see the deadline coming in, you feel you must write as much as possible, so you just start. It turns out that this is a pitfall, you will end up wasting precious time. This is because, in the rush of writing, you will start making mistakes and will need to backtrack. Also, as you push yourself to write more and more, you realize that there are many blind spots in your knowledge, and have to stop to do some research. You write, but end up in many cul-de-sacs that force you to do multiple passes, and delays accumulate. The good practice, instead, would be to slow down on the writing, and spend more time first into clarifying what each section should contain. When this is sorted out, then you write, and move on. This is a sustainable work process that can get you somewhere (hopefully).

The story above represents, of course, my own experience in this very moment of my life. The second part of the paragraph is the workflow I am trying to implement right now, to "do things righ". Maybe this situation is too subjective, but I think this can be generalized to work in general. Imagine for example a software developer needing to push out important updates. You would think that he should be consuming his hands on the keyboard at all times of the day, to code as much as possible. But in reality, what he should be doing is studying the problem, obtain a deep understanding of the issue, construct a mental model of the solution. Only then, the solution should be implemented. I cannot think of a work that does not benefit from thinking as much as possible before executing.

Not doing this is what I would call blind execution. We are told to fill our agendae with things to do, while we should fill them with things to think. The execution should follow some solution we have figured out about the problem at hand. It takes a certain courage to follow through with this work flow. It is unintuitive because uou feel that you should do the task at hand. Instead, the best outcome comes from putting execution after clarity and respecting this rationale religiously. No clarity, no execution. It is a work principle, a fundamental of work process.

This blog is titled like this because blind execution represents a bottleneck. Acting before thinking is a losing strategy: problems will be encountered systematically, requiring to go back to the blackboard and start from scratch again. This bottleneck is removed by back-loading the work with a good dose of thinking. To summarize with one of many quips my supervisor told me throughout my PhD: "less doing, more thinking." I did not get what he this meant then, while I think I get it now.
